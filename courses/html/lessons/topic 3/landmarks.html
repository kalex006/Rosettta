3.2 Landmark Elements

The Cardinal Points: Navigating the topographical regions of the interface.

The Concept of Landmarks

Landmarks are specific regions of a page that screen readers use to "teleport" users around. Instead of tabbing through 50 links, a user can ask to "Go to Navigation" or "Go to Footer."

We define these primarily using <header>, <nav>, and <footer>.

The Scoped Header

A common misconception is that a page can only have one <header>. In reality, the Header element is contextual.

  • Global Header: Inside <body>. Contains the logo and main navigation.
  • Section Header: Inside <article> or <section>. Contains the title of that specific piece of content and its author byline.
HTML header_scoping.html 
<body>
    <!-- The Site Header -->
    <header>
        <h1>ROSETTA</h1>
    </header>

    <article>
        <!-- The Article Header -->
        <header>
            <h2>Deep Dive into DOM</h2>
            <p>By Kalex</p>
        </header>
        <p>Content...</p>
    </article>
</body>

The Navigation <nav>

The <nav> element is reserved for major blocks of navigation links.

Do not wrap every list of links in a nav tag. A list of social icons in the footer or a list of "related posts" usually does not qualify. Overusing landmarks creates "noise" for assistive technology users.

The Footer & Ancestry

Like the header, the <footer> is scoped to its nearest Sectioning Root or Sectioning Content.

A footer inside an <article> typically contains the publication date, tags, and bio of the author. A footer inside the <body> contains the copyright notice and contact info for the entire site.

The <address> Anomaly

The <address> element is often misunderstood. It is not for arbitrary postal addresses. It is specifically for the contact information of the document owner.

It should generally be placed inside the <footer>.

HTML contact_info.html 
<footer>
    <p>© 2026 Project Rosetta</p>
    <address>
        Contact the architect at <a href="mailto:arch@rosetta.dev">arch@rosetta.dev</a>
    </address>
</footer>
⚠
Architect's Tip: Avoid putting <header> or <footer> inside another <header> or <footer>. While the parser might accept it, it breaks the semantic model.
